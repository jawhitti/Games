# PAPER VICTORY: KINGS AND POPES

*A game of hostage courts, borrowed crowns, and a victory that calls for your head.*

For 6 players. About 60-75 minutes.

> This is the **delegate** evolution of *Paper Victory: The Schism*. The earlier
> `RULEBOOK.md` (the abstract terror core, kneel-tokens and secret ballots) still
> stands as its own lean game. This one keeps the same soul -- two thrones, one
> must die -- but replaces tokens with **family**, and builds the whole machine
> on the loop where funding your victory is what gets you killed.

---

## The curse

You can play as a **House** (most of you) or, if you dare, as the **King** or the
**Pope**. The thrones look like the winning seats. They are the death seats.

A throne wins by finishing its **monument** -- the King his Castle, the Pope his
Cathedral. But a monument is expensive, and the only way to fund it fast is to
squeeze the Houses: call in their debts, and when they cannot pay, **kill the
kin they left in your keeping and seize their lands.** Every coin that finishes
the monument is a House you have gutted. And the finished monument is what calls
the **Reckoning** -- the moment the court you brutalized gets to vote on whether
you live.

> Congratulations. You're king. You're fucked.

The Houses are not safe either. To grow, a House must take lands it cannot
afford -- on credit, with a child posted to the throne's court as collateral. If
you back the throne that falls, that child dies. If you starve yourself liquid to
stay safe, you never grow and you never win. Everyone is leveraged. Everyone has
someone in the fire.

---

## Components

- **2 Throne boards**: the **King** (the Castle, his Court) and the **Pope** (the
  Cathedral, Rome).
- **Monument tracks**, one per throne (fill to complete).
- **Delegate figures**: **3 per House**, each a named member of the family.
- **The two Courts**: board zones where delegates are posted -- the King's Court
  and Rome. A delegate is at home, at Court, or at Rome.
- **Holding cards**: lands and titles a throne can grant (Estates from the King,
  Benefices and Relics from the Pope). Each has a **price** and a **score value**.
- **Coin** (scarce on purpose).
- **Debt markers** (a House's IOU to a throne, equal to a holding's price).
- **Loyalty tokens**: each posted delegate secretly holds **LOYAL** or **TURNED**.
- **Agenda cards**: one secret win-condition per House -- Protector, Magnate,
  Zealot, Regicide (see *Winning*).

---

## Setup

1. Seat 6. Assign or draft the two thrones; the other four are Houses.
2. Each House takes **3 delegates** (name them -- it matters later) and a small
   starting purse (**4 coin**, playtest default).
3. **Deal each House one secret Agenda card** (Protector / Magnate / Zealot /
   Regicide). A Zealot also secretly notes *which* throne it backs.
4. Each throne sets its monument track to **0**. The Castle / Cathedral is a
   **long build** (cost **90**, playtest default) -- finishing it should take most
   of the game.
5. Shuffle the Holding decks, one per throne.
6. The game runs in rounds until a throne **completes its monument**, a House
   **calls a rebellion**, or the **round cap** (24) is reached -- any of which
   triggers the Reckoning. **No rebellion may be called before the back half of
   the game** (round 12): the early game is a guaranteed simmer, and the whispers
   must be earned before anyone dares flip the table.

---

## What a delegate is (all at once)

A single delegate is four things, and you can only use them as one:

1. **Your public allegiance** -- where they're posted, in plain view. A delegate
   at Court backs the Crown; at Rome, the Mitre. Everyone sees it.
2. **Your score** -- a delegate who comes home alive is worth a lot, and carries
   home the title they secured. A murdered delegate is worth nothing and takes
   their holding with them.
3. **Your collateral** -- the security a throne holds against the lands it lent
   you. Your kin is the throne's hostage.
4. **A knife** -- a delegate posted at a court is close enough to **move against**
   the throne they serve (see *Betrayal*).

You can't spend the same delegate twice. Every choice for one of these is a
sacrifice of the others. That tension is the game.

---

## A round

### 1. Income
Every House takes **2 coin**. (Thrones earn nothing they don't extract -- that's
the point.)

### 2. The courts grant lands (gift-as-leash)
A throne may offer a **Holding** to a House. A House that wants the score but
can't afford the price **buys it on credit**: take the Holding, place a **debt
marker** equal to its price, and **post one delegate** to that throne's court as
collateral. The land is yours to score -- *if* you ever clear the debt or your
hostage walks out of the Reckoning alive.

The throne's generosity is a rope. You are now leveraged to its survival.

### 3. The thrones press the Houses
A throne wants money for its monument and bodies for its hall. It has three moves,
escalating in drama and in the fury they earn:

- **(a) A gift for cash or a hostage** -- the gift-as-leash deal above. The throne
  *gives* land to *get* coin or a posted hostage. The House partly consents (it got
  something). **Lowest heat.**
- **(b) A naked demand for cash** -- extortion, nothing given back. Fast money when
  Houses are flush; useless against the broke. **More heat per coin.**
- **(c) A summons -- "send a child to my court"** -- demand a hostage outright, give
  nothing. The terror tool: take a body for a vote, a shield, an example, or to drag
  a suspected knife close where you can watch or kill it. A throne tends to summon
  from the House **hoarding the most kin at home** -- it wants your daughter, not
  your stable boy. **Maximum heat.**

This is the **whim**: a throne presses *whenever it likes*, and the threat alone
bends the room.

**Calling a debt (the margin call).** When a throne calls a debt secured by a
hostage: the House **pays in full** (kin home, clean title, coin to the monument),
or it **defaults** -- the throne **kills the hostage and seizes the land**, the kin
dead and the Holding's value poured into the build.

**Refusing a demand (the ladder).** When a House refuses a naked demand it cannot
or will not meet:

1. **The throne takes back a gift** -- a Holding it granted reverts (the House loses
   that score; the land funds the monument).
2. **If there is no gift left to return**, the House must choose: **surrender a
   hostage** (a child goes to court, or to the block), **or call the rebellion
   right now** (see the Reckoning -- a House can trigger it, but not before the
   back half of the game).

So a throne grinds a House through its lands first, then its children -- and a
House stripped bare can hand over kin or **flip the table.**

### 4. Loyalties settle
Each posted delegate's holder secretly sets or updates their **LOYAL / TURNED**
token. You'll want to turn when the throne has wronged you or is going down; you'll
want to stay loyal when it's winning and has treated you well. Nobody sees your
choice until the Reckoning.

### 5. Check the monuments
If a track hits **24**, that throne has its paper victory -- and triggers the
**Reckoning** immediately. Otherwise, next round.

## Brutality and fury (the table decides)

When a throne kills a hostage, it does **not** anger everyone equally. Fury is
**personal and local**:

- The House whose child just died wants the throne dead. Obviously.
- But a House whose **rival** just lost an heir and a holding might be *delighted*
  -- one less competitor, a neighbor cut down. It may kneel deeper.
- And a House watching the precedent -- *he kills kin, casually, and mine are in
  his keeping too* -- may turn out of pure fear, next on the list.

Same axe, opposite readings, and only the table knows which. Worse for the
throne: **the suspicions are yours to feed.** *"Sire, it's House Vargo whose
loyalty you should doubt -- call their debt."* Houses aim the throne's brutality
at each other, steering the axe toward rivals and away from their own blood. The
throne thinks it is choosing whom to crush. It is being pointed.

There is no fury track to tally. The fury lives in the **LOYAL / TURNED** tokens
and in the talking. That is the whole game, and no rule can write it for you.

---

## Betrayal

A delegate posted at a court is close enough to strike. At the Reckoning, a
**TURNED** delegate is one who moved against the throne they served. The
consequence is a clean inversion of loyalty -- and it's the same table that
governs a collateral hostage's fate:

| Your delegate at a throne's court | The throne **stands** | The throne **falls** |
|---|---|---|
| **stayed LOYAL** | lives -> comes home, **you get title, debt paid** | dies -> **lose the delegate and the holding** |
| **moved against it (TURNED)** | caught -> **purged in the throne's own hall**, lose delegate and holding | spared -> comes home, **you get title, debt paid** |

So loyalty pays off only if your throne survives; betrayal pays off only if it
falls. From the outside the kneeler and the assassin look identical. A throne sits
among its "supporters" unable to tell the knives from the knees -- and the more
kin it has dragged into its court through debt, the more hands there are that
might move. **Consolidation breeds the coup.**

---

## The Reckoning

**Three things can trigger it:** a throne **completes its monument** (its paper
victory), a cornered House **calls the rebellion** (refused a demand with nothing
left to give -- only in the back half of the game), or the **round cap** is
reached. In play, the three split roughly evenly, and a typical game runs about
**20 rounds** before one of them fires.

For each throne, judge it by its own court: count the posted delegates' tokens.

- **More TURNED than LOYAL -> the throne FALLS.** The rebellion succeeds; the
  monument-builder goes to the block.
- **More LOYAL than TURNED -> the throne STANDS.**

### The rival piles on
When the Reckoning is a **rebellion against one throne**, the *other* throne
chooses: **pile on, or stand clear.** A rival who reads itself safe will happily
throw its weight behind the revolt -- being rid of your only competitor is the
surest path to ruling alone. Piling on **adds the rival's faction to the knives**
(it can topple a throne the court alone could not). But it is a gamble: **if the
coup fails anyway, the crown that survives destroys the rival who backed it.** The
Pope who reaches for the King's throat and misses loses his own.

### The weathers
The schism still bites -- across the two thrones the outcome is one of three:

- **One throne falls (the common case).** It dies; the other is confirmed.
- **Both fall.** No sovereign survives. The courts collapse together.
- **Both stand.** A deadlock the realm cannot hold -- both survive the night and
  turn on the whole court (see scoring).

Then resolve **every posted delegate** by the betrayal table (their fate follows
whether *their* throne stood or fell), bring the dead off the board, and score.

## The castle is the gallows

Here is the loop, start to finish, because it is the entire design:

> The throne needs coin for its monument -> it calls debts -> the Houses that
> can't pay lose kin and land -> the throne gets its coin and its seized lands ->
> the monument completes -> **the completed monument triggers the Reckoning** ->
> the court it butchered to fund the build votes it onto the block.

A throne **cannot fund its victory without building its own gallows.** The
monument's completion and the throne's execution are, by the rules, the *same
event.* This is not flavor. It is the win condition wired directly to the death.
You win paper. The paper kills you.

The throne's whole problem is to thread it: build fast enough to finish, gently
enough to survive the court that finishing summons. The needle is thin. It is
meant to be.

---

## Winning

Each House holds a **secret agenda** -- its own definition of victory, dealt at
setup and never revealed. More than one House can satisfy its agenda; some games
no House does. After the weather resolves and the dead are removed, reveal agendas
and check them. The four (deal one each; with more Houses, repeat):

| Agenda | Wins if... | so it plays... |
|---|---|---|
| **The Protector** | all three kin are **alive** *and* you hold land from **both** crowns | hedges -- a foot in each court, commits to neither, shepherds the family through |
| **The Magnate** | you end **richest at the table** (coin + lands); kin don't matter | leverages hard, *feeds* children to the thrones for profit, spends blood like coin |
| **The Zealot** | your chosen throne achieves its **paper victory** (finishes *and* stands) | commits utterly to one crown, props up its build, prays it threads the needle |
| **The Regicide** | a **rebellion succeeds** -- an uprising topples a crown (not a throne tripping over its own monument; the *revolt* must win) | agitates, turns its own kin to knives, stokes and joins every revolt it can. Chaos is a ladder. |

These pull in different directions on purpose: the Magnate sells the daughter the
Protector is bleeding to save; the Zealot needs the very monument the Regicide is
trying to bury under a revolt. Same table, four different wars.

> In the **Both Stand** weather, both thrones purge the court: every *posted*
> delegate is held for sedition and lost -- a catastrophe that guts the Protector
> and the leveraged, and that only the Regicide (who wanted a crown to fall) and
> the cautious can profit from.

**A throne** wins only if it **completes its monument AND stands** at the
Reckoning -- the paper victory, actually survived. A throne that completes and
falls is **guillotined** (the purest loss in the game). A throne couped before it
finishes, or one that never finishes, does not win.

## Strategy notes

- **As a House:** leverage is growth and growth is exposure. The 2-1 split (two
  kin at the throne you read winning, one hedged at the other) buys you a floor
  and costs you a ceiling. Going all-in on one court is the high-variance line:
  triumph or a wiped family. Keep *some* coin against the whim, or you're a
  hostage to the King's mood.
- **Aim the axe.** You cannot stop the King killing -- but you can often choose
  *who.* Feed him the rival you want gone.
- **The doomed delegate is an asset.** A child already bound for the block is
  cheap to spend, cheap to turn. Cash them, flip them, get *something* before the
  axe.
- **As a throne:** every hostage you kill is a knife you might have needed and an
  enemy you certainly made. Brutality is a loan against your own neck. Read which
  Houses' fury helps you (rivals glad of a culling) and which damns you (the
  fearful, the bereaved). And finish before the room finishes you.

## Designer's notes

This is the full delegate game. Its companion model, `courts.js`, simulates the
**structural loop** with naive, self-interested bots, and the build of it taught
the design three things:

1. **Mechanically, brutality pays -- until the court can fight back.** With nothing
   but debt-calling, a throne that brutalizes to fund its monument *finishes before
   the fury organizes* and stands ~90% of the time. The castle-as-gallows is not an
   automatic mechanical truth; it needs the court to *act.*
2. **House-triggered rebellion plus the rival piling on is what makes it bite.** Let
   a cornered House detonate the Reckoning at the moment of maximum fury, and let the
   other crown seize that moment to be rid of its competitor, and a brutal throne's
   death rate climbs from ~3% to ~30% -- toppled mid-build by a revolt its rival
   joined. The gallows are real once *rivalry*, not altruism, carries the rope.
3. **The four agendas balance** to a ~40% win rate each, but only once each agenda's
   *behavior* matches its goal (the Protector had to be made a hedger, not a turtle;
   the Zealot tied to a throne that actually *wins*).

What the model **cannot** show is the game itself -- the player-relative fury, the
whispered denunciations, the aiming of the axe, the coordinated outrage that turns a
court. Every number above is a *floor*: a real table, lying and scheming, makes the
fog thicker and the gallows hungrier than any bot can. Run `node courts.js --story
<seed>` to watch one Castle rise on dead children and call its builder to the block;
`node courts.js` for the brutality sweep; `--costsweep` for the monument-cost lever.
