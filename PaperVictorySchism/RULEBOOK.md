# PAPER VICTORY: THE SCHISM

*A game of public loyalty, secret conviction, and the certainty that someone at the top must die.*

For 6 players. About 45-60 minutes.

---

## The pitch

The King and the Pope both claim to rule. The realm cannot hold two crowns, so
at the end of the game there is a **Reckoning** -- and one of them goes to the
block. Maybe both. Maybe, against all sense, neither.

You are not the King or the Pope. You are a **House** -- a noble family that
must survive whatever comes. Every round you kneel to one throne in full view of
the table (the **public record**), and in secret you cast a vote that will
decide who actually holds power (your **conviction**). Those two things do not
have to match. They usually shouldn't.

When the Reckoning falls, the throne the table secretly chose survives. The
other one dies -- and the survivor purges everyone who was publicly kneeling to
the loser. The trick: you don't control the vote, only your sliver of it, and
you must read where the room is actually going while everyone lies to your face.

> Congratulations -- you have a throne to bow to. You're fucked.

---

## Components

- **2 Throne boards**: the **Crown** (King) and the **Mitre** (Pope).
- **1 Public Record** track per House (a row of tokens, Crown side / Mitre side).
- **Conviction ballots**: small cards marked **CROWN**, **MITRE**, and
  **NEITHER**. Each House holds a full set of all three, every round.
- **1 ballot box** (any opaque container).
- **Agenda cards** (one dealt secretly to each House -- see *Agendas*).
- **Realm Treasury track** (a single shared dial, starts at **30**).
- **Living / Purged** markers.

No money changes hands between players in this version. The Treasury is a single
shared number that only matters for two of the agendas.

---

## Setup

1. Seat 6 players. Two take the thrones: one is the **King** (Crown), one is the
   **Pope** (Mitre). The other four are **Houses**.
   *(Thrones can be assigned, drafted, or volunteered for. Being a throne is a
   different game -- see "Playing a throne" below.)*
2. Set the **Realm Treasury** to **30**.
3. Deal each **House** one secret **Agenda card**. Do not reveal it. Ever, if you
   can help it.
4. Give each House a full set of conviction ballots (Crown / Mitre / Neither).
5. The game lasts **6 rounds**, then the Reckoning.

---

## A round

Each round has three steps, in order.

### 1. The realm bleeds

Lower the **Realm Treasury** by **3 to 5** (roll a die, or just step it down by 4
if you want no luck). Wars, tithes, and bad harvests. The realm is always poorer
at the end than the beginning -- the only question is how poor.

### 2. The Houses kneel (public)

Going around the table, each House publicly declares a throne -- **Crown** or
**Mitre** -- and places a token on that side of its Public Record. This is
visible to everyone and **permanent**: it is the historical record of who you
were seen to support. You may kneel to a different throne in a later round, but
you can never erase a token already placed.

This is the table-talk step. Argue. Promise. Threaten. A House deciding who to
kneel to is the heart of the negotiation: the thrones want your visible loyalty,
your rivals want you exposed on the losing side, and you want to look loyal to
whoever is actually going to win -- which nobody knows yet.

### 3. The Houses convict (secret)

Every House -- *and both thrones* -- secretly drops **one** conviction ballot
into the box: **CROWN**, **MITRE**, or **NEITHER**.

- A throne almost always votes for itself (it is fighting for its life), but it
  is not required to.
- A House votes its conscience, its agenda, or its read of the room. Your secret
  vote does **not** have to match the throne you just publicly knelt to. In fact
  the gap between your public record and your secret vote is the entire game.

The ballots stay in the box until the Reckoning. They are not counted between
rounds. You are voting blind, repeatedly, on an outcome you can only guess at.

> **The escape hatch.** Any House may, on any round, cast **NEITHER** instead of
> a throne. This is the desperate move of a House that knows it is publicly
> trapped on the losing side: if enough players vote NEITHER, *both* thrones
> fall and the purge never happens (see *Both Die*). It is a blind hail-mary --
> you are gambling that the rest of the table is as cornered as you are. Throw it
> too early and you help no one; throw it too late and you're already on the
> block.

After 6 rounds, open the box.

---

## The Reckoning

Count every ballot ever cast across all 6 rounds. You have three totals:
**Crown (C)**, **Mitre (M)**, **Neither (N)**. Compare them to find the
**weather**:

### One Throne Dies  (the common case, ~90%)
If one throne clearly outpolls the other (their gap is **more than 6**) **and**
NEITHER did not win, that throne is **legitimate**. The other throne is the
**pretender** and **goes to the block**.

Then the survivor **purges the court**: every House whose Public Record shows
**more tokens for the pretender than for the legitimate throne** is **Purged**
(out of the game, no win). Houses who were seen leaning the right way, or who
stayed balanced, **live**.

> This is the engine. Loyalty to a losing throne is lethal. The survivor doesn't
> care what you *secretly* voted -- only what the record shows you *did*.

### Both Die  ("a plague on both your houses", ~5%)
If **NEITHER** outpolls both thrones, the schism consumes them. **Both the King
and the Pope go to the block.** With no surviving sovereign, **there is no
purge** -- the entire court walks free into the ashes. **Amnesty.**

### Both Live  (the deadlock, ~5%)
If the two thrones finish within **6 votes of each other** (and NEITHER did not
win), the vote is too close to depose anyone. **Both thrones survive the
night -- and turn as one on the court.** Every House is held to account for its
sedition and apostasy: **all Houses are Purged.** No House can win in this
weather. (The thrones can -- see below.)

> Both Live is the rule that punishes good play. "Kneel safely, vote safely, keep
> your head down" is a strong line -- until the 1-in-20 night when keeping your
> head down gets it taken anyway. It exists so that no cautious strategy is ever
> truly safe.

---

## Winning

After the weather resolves and the Purged are removed, check agendas. **Any
House that survived and met its Agenda wins.** More than one House can win. It is
entirely possible for *no* House to win (e.g. Both Live).

### The thrones win conditions
- A throne **wins** if it survives the Reckoning (One Throne, on the winning
  side; or Both Live). A throne that goes to the block **loses**, always.
- Being a throne is a coin flip on survival and a bullseye on your back: you
  cannot hide your allegiance, you are the thing the Houses are voting on, and
  half the time you die no matter how well you played. That is the point.

### Agendas (the Houses' secret win conditions)
Each House is dealt one. You win only if you **survive** *and* your agenda is
satisfied (except the reformer, whose agenda is its own survival clause).

| Agenda | Wins if... |
|---|---|
| **Zealot of the Crown** | the Crown is legitimate (One Throne, Crown wins) and you live |
| **Zealot of the Mitre** | the Mitre is legitimate (One Throne, Mitre wins) and you live |
| **Reformer** | **Both Die** (the schism wins). You burn it all down. |
| **Ascetic** | the realm ends **poor** (Treasury at or below **12**) and you live |
| **Magnate** | the realm ends **rich** (Treasury above **12**) and you live |
| **Survivor** | you simply live, whatever the weather (unless Both Live takes you) |

> Agendas are what make the plotting real. A Zealot of the Crown and a Reformer
> at the same table are working toward opposite Reckonings while both kneeling
> wherever it's safe. The Ascetic wants the realm bled white; the Magnate wants
> it hoarded -- they fight over the Treasury without ever naming the fight. You
> never reveal your agenda. Half the game is guessing everyone else's.

---

## Playing a throne

If you hold the Crown or the Mitre, your game is different:

- You cannot lie about your allegiance -- you *are* an allegiance.
- You want Houses to publicly kneel to you (it shapes who gets purged for you if
  you win) **and** to secretly convict for you (that's what keeps you alive).
  Those are two separate sells.
- You will spend the game courting, bargaining, and threatening Houses whose
  votes you cannot see and whose agendas you do not know.
- And roughly half the time, you will do everything right and still go to the
  block, because the room decided your rival was more useful alive. Make your
  peace with it.

---

## Strategy notes (for new tables)

- **Your public record is a debt, not an asset.** Every token is evidence. Kneel
  toward the throne you believe will *win*, not the one you *like* -- unless your
  agenda needs the like.
- **Watch the gap.** If the room is piling onto one throne, the Reckoning is
  going to be a clean purge and you do **not** want to be the House holding
  pretender tokens. If the room is split down the middle, fear **Both Live**.
- **NEITHER is coordination without conversation.** You cannot announce "let's
  burn them both" -- but four ballots can say it silently. Reformers live for
  this. Cornered Houses pray for it.
- **The thrones lie too.** A throne is allowed to vote NEITHER, or even for its
  rival, if it has decided it would rather take the whole table down than lose
  alone. Trust nothing.

## Designer's notes

This rulebook describes the **terror core** of Paper Victory: two thrones, each
about 50% doomed (one must die), a three-way secret vote, three weathers, an
escape hatch, and hidden agendas as win conditions.

A companion Monte Carlo simulator (`terror.js`) plays the skeleton with naive
bots and confirms the structure tunes cleanly:

- the three weathers land near **90 / 5 / 5** (one throne / both die / both live);
- each throne dies almost exactly **50%** of the time;
- the escape hatch fires rarely (well under one flip per game) -- a true
  hail-mary, not a routine move;
- the Ascetic and Magnate agendas are clean coin-flips against each other.

The simulator is also honest about its blind spot: bots cannot **bluff,
whisper, or pressure**, so they bandwagon onto the obvious winner, which makes
the purge toothless and survival too cheap in the model. That gap is not a bug in
the game -- it *is* the game. The lying, the feinting, the table-talk that the
bots can't do is exactly the layer the humans bring. Run `node terror.js --story
<seed>` to watch a single court rise and fall; the chronicles where the public
record and the secret vote point in opposite directions are the ones that show
what the table is really for.
